The Building Blocks: Classifying Fibers

By Length

Staple Fibers

Short fibers, measured in millimeters or inches. All natural fibers except silk are staple fibers. They create softer, fuzzier yarns.

Filament Fibers

Long, continuous fibers measured in meters or yards. Silk and all man-made fibers are filaments. They produce smoother, more lustrous yarns.

By Source

Natural Fibers

Sourced from plants, animals, or minerals found in nature.

Man-Made Fibers

Synthesized from chemical processes, often using petrochemicals or regenerated natural materials like wood pulp.

Fibers are chosen for their unique combination of properties. Below, we compare the primary performance characteristics of Cotton and Linen.

A Tale of Two Fibers: Cotton vs. Linen

Cotton: The King of Fibers

Derived from the seed pod of the cotton plant, this fiber is celebrated for its comfort and versatility. Its natural twist, called convolution, helps fibers cohere into strong yarn.

Favorable Properties

  • Highly Absorbent & Breathable
  • Soft and Comfortable
  • Good Strength
  • Easy to Clean & Dye

Unfavorable Properties

  • Wrinkles Easily
  • Low Luster
  • Susceptible to Mildew

The Power of One Cotton Bale

Linen: The Ancient Staple

Harvested from the stem of the flax plant, linen is one of the oldest textiles known to humankind. It is prized for its exceptional coolness and fresh feel in hot weather.

Favorable Properties

  • Excellent Strength (Strongest Veg. Fiber)
  • Very Absorbent & Quick-Drying
  • Naturally Lustrous
  • Becomes Softer with Use

Unfavorable Properties

  • Wrinkles Very Easily
  • Less Elasticity
  • Can be Brittle

From Flax to Linen: Key Steps

  1. 1
    Retting

    Microbes and moisture break down the plant's woody stem.

  2. 2
    Scutching

    The woody stalk is crushed and removed to release the fibers.

  3. 3
    Hackling

    Fibers are combed to separate long staples from short tow.
